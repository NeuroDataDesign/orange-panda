Stimulus – Evoked Activity

  • Pros

  • Cons

  • Co-adaptive BCI

Invasive BCI – Population Decoding

  • Decode control signals

Experiment 2: Nicoleis

  • Control of robotic arm by monkeys based on spikes spontaneously from:
    • Primary motor cortex
    • Dorsal premotor cortex
    • Posterior parietal cortex
  • 2 movements:
    • 1-Dimensional left-to-right movement
    • 3-Dimensional hand movement across a tray

Linear Regression Algorithm

  • v(t) = recorded hand position
  • u(t) = recorded neural activity
  • w(t) = weight vectors, c = intercept
    • These 2 calculated from training data
  • m and n define how much data used for prediction
    • m = 0 when predicting in real time

Neural Network

  • Same input of neural activity
  • Hidden layer of 15-20 sigmoid functions
  • Linear output
    • 1-Output for 1-Dimensional
    • 3-Output for 3-Dimensional

1-Dimensional Predictions

  • Predictions were close by both methods
  • Captured by correlation coefficient r
  • Improved in first few minutes
  • Remained stable at average values between 0.6 and 0.7

3-Dimensional Predictions

  • Again close, but significantly worse at endpoints of movement
  • Improvement in prediction accuracy over time
  • Predictions for targets oriented to the right could help predict the left

Experiment 3: Schwartz, Velliste

  • Cortical signals to control multi-jointed prosthetic device
  • Monkey self-fed via neural control of a prosthetic arm
  • 4 Directions of movement for arm:
    • X, Y, Z directions
    • Aperture distance (finger grip width)

Learning Algorithms

  • Output has 4 Dimensions:
    • Velocity of X, Y, and Z
    • Aperture velocity
  • 4D vector calculated via weighted sum of neurons
    • Weight = firing rate
    • Weights inputted in similar eq to previous linear model
  • Final velocity vector integrated to find endpoint positions
  • Converted to commands for robot hand

In Practice

  • 2 days of continuous self-feeding with prosthetic arm
  • Success rate:
    • 61%
    • 116 primary cortex neurons
    • For just positioning, 98% success rate
  • Kalman filter has potential for improvement, as uses a probabilistic model

Experiment 5: Donoghue, Vajas-Irwin

  • Map neural activity in primary cortex of monkeys to arm, wrist, and hand posture
  • Linear model predicted 25 joint angles
  • Training data = neural firing rates from 30-122 neurons
  • Actual angles captured via reflective markers

Algorithm

  • x = unknown time-varying hidden state vector
  • f = firing rates of neurons
  • y = kinematic outputs
  • n,m = zero-mean Gaussians representing noise processes

Training

  • A, B, C, Q, and R trained via likely values and gradient descent methods
  • Trained angles, (x, y, z) arm endpoint, and grip aperture
  • Mean correlation coefficient joint angle = .72 +- .094
    • Thus, sufficient info in several tens of motor cortical neurons to reconstruct reaching and grasping

Estimating Kinetic Parameters from Neural Activity

  • Kinematic parameters: positions + joint angles
  • Kinetic parameters: force + joint torque

Experiment 6: Hatsopoulos, Fagg

  • Incorporating kinetics
  • Using kinematic readings and equations of motion, calculate kinetics
  • Linear filter to predict torque based on neural activity up to one second in the past
  • Found similar efficacy to kinematic predictions

Local Field Potentials (LFP)

  • Local areas summed potential rather than specific spikes
  • Electrode array placed far from any individual neuron
  • Kalman filters based on different frequencies isolated in LFPs tested for 3-dimensional hand position and grip aperture
  • High frequency bands had highest correlation

  • Tracked via 2 cameras
  • Recorded neural and kinematic data used to learn a linear model

Experiment Results

  • Kinematics of walking could be predicted reasonably well
  • Trained model also predicted muscle activation during walking recorded via EMG
  • Correlation coefficients were in range of 0.2 - 0.9

    • Foot Contact 0.58-0.61
    • Walking Speed 0.24-0.42
    • Step Frequency 0.48-0.57
    • Step Length 0.30-0.40

Cursor Control

  • control of computer cursors using motor neurons
  • allow locked-in patients to access menus
  • Two types of cursor control:
    • 1. Linear control
      • monkeys use 7-30 primary motor neurons to move mouse cursor
      • uses hand to track continuous target, following the trajectory, then replace with neural control
      • hand or neural control had around the same reaction time

2. Nonlinear Kalman Filter

  • two monkeys were given two tasks using a joystick:
    • i. "center-out"- move cursor from center to a random target
    • ii. "pursuit" - monkeys track continuously moving targets
  • 94-240 neurons, recorded via multielectrode array in: primary motor cortex (M1), primary somatosensory cortex (S1), dorsal premotor cortex (PMd), posterior parietal cortex (PP), supplementary motor area (SMA)
  • neural data used to train nonlinear version of Kalman filter, known as unscented Kalman filter (UKF)

The UKF allows for better tuning due to its quadratic nature. It also uses position and velocity values from 10 consecutive time steps for AR.

Cognitive BCIs

BCIs with higher level cognitive signals

Reaching Movements

  • neural activity of parietal reach region (PRR) of cerebral cortex can decode the target location of intended reaching movement
  • monkey reach at a flashed location after a brief delay period- Bayesian algorithm
  • either normally , or via "brain control"
  • Haar wavelet- signal over interval represented by coefficients
  • shows only ~60-68% accuracy, but with LFP stimulation along with both spikes, might have 86%

Enhancing Cognitive BCI

  • There's Tskip and Tint, Tskip occurs when the information is not yet reliable and Tint shows up for interval for target.
  • target response was predicted using spike counts
  • overall performance measured by ITR
  • best = 6.5 bps for 8 target, translate to 15 words per minute

Invasive BCIs in Human

Cursor and Robotic Control using a Multielectrode Array Implant

  • BrainGate sensor, 100 silicon microelectrodes implanted in arm area of primary motor cortex
  • But can motor intention still modulate cortical activity three years after spinal cord injury and absence of hand motion?
  • imagined hand motion will activate some neurons!
  • can move cursor in the general direction but not exactly accurate
  • eventually develops wider scopes such as simulated email, draw using paint program, adjust volume, play video games, etc

Cognitive BCIs in Humans

  • recall neural activity from human primary motor cortex can control trajectory of cursors
  • however, it's the frontal cortex beyond primary motor cortex that is related to planning and initiating movement direction
  • this plan worked in monkeys
  • still not been tested in depth yet, but positively thought of as to work.

Long-Term Use of Invasive BCIs

  • for the two monkey experiment regarding the center-out reaching task, the 128-electrode array remained in the monkey's brains for 19 days.
  • it is proven that the longer the monkeys used the device, the higher the accuracy of the task and the lower the reaction time will be.
  • if a decoder is shuffled, then humans can take an adjusting period to get used to the new decoder and achieve the same accurate results.

Long-Term Use of a Human BCI Implant

  • neuro point-and-click experiment for five consecutive days
  • based on spikes of motor neurons to determine two-dimensional cursor velocity
  • 94.9% accuracy for center-out task and 91.9% accuracy for the random target task
  • other interferences were measured such as electrode impedances, neural spike waveforms, and local field potentials to rule out discrepancy
