Chapter 2

Basic Neuroscience

Basic Neuroscience

  • Neuron
    • Receives info from hundreds of neurons
    • Processes the info
    • Conveys output to hundreds of neurons
  • Plastic Connections
    • Allows brain to adapt to new inputs

Von Neumann Architecture

  • CPU
  • Memory
  • Fixed connections between components
  • Serial mode of computation

Neuron

  • Impermeable lipid bi-layer
  • Ionic channels
  • Potential difference of ~ -70mV across membrane

“Brain Computer Interfacing.” By Rajesh P. N. Rao

Action Potential (Spike)

  • Rapid influx of Na+ into cell
    • Rise in membrane potential
  • K+ Channels Open
    • Rapid outflow of K+ ions
    • Drop in membrane potential
  • Info is conveyed by the firing rate
    • # spikes per second
    • Timing of spikes

Dendrites and Axons

  • Spike initiated near soma/axon junction
  • Axon emanates output spike to other neurons
  • Myelin sheath boosts speed of propagation over long distances

https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/1206_The_Neuron.jpg

Synapses

  • Gap (synaptic cleft) between axon of a neuron (presynaptic neuron)
  • And another soma (postsynaptic neuron)
  • Action potential releases neurotransmitters into cleft
    • NT bind to ion channels in soma
    • Channels open -> Local membrane potential changes
  • Excitatory – increase in membrane potential
  • Inhibitory – decrease in membrane potential
  • Neurons are one or the other, never both

Threshold Model of Spike Generation

  • Neuron receives sufficiently strong inputs from synapses for membrane to cross a specific threshold
    • Results in spike
  • 0/1 inputs are converted to analog changes in local membrane potential
    • Changes are summed at the soma
    • If summation exceeds threshold -> Spike occurs

Synaptic Plasticity

  • Long Term Potentiation (LTP)
    • Related to Hebbian Learning
    • If neuron A consistently causes neuron B to fire, then the strength of the connection from A to B should be increased
    • Hippocampus and neocortex
  • Long Term Depression (LTD)
    • Decrease in strength of synaptic connection, caused by uncorrelated firing between two neurons
    • Hippocampus and neocortex

Spike Time Dependent Plasticity (STDP)

  • Hebbian STDP
    • Presynaptic spike occurs (1-40ms) before postsynaptic spike à Strengthened synapse
    • Vise Versa (1-40ms after)
    • Cerebral cortex / Hippocampus
  • Anti-Hebbian STDP
    • Presynaptic spike occurs after post synaptic spike -> Strengthened synapse
    • Vise Versa
    • Inhibitory synapses in cerebellum

Short-Term Facilitation and Depression

  • The only type of Short Term plasticity
    • If neuron receives a burst of spikes as input:
    • STD
      • First spike has most effect
      • Those that follow have lesser and lesser changes in membrane potential until an equilibrium point is reached
    • STF
    • Each successive spike has a larger effect than its predecessor until a saturation point is reached
  • In Neocortical synapses

Nervous System

  • Central Nervous System (CNS)
    • Brain and spinal cord
    • Spinal cord sends info to/from brain
      • Also has local feedback loops for reflexes
  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
    • Somatic Nervous System
      • Neurons connected to skeletal muscles, skin, and organs
    • Autonomic Nervous System
      • Neurons controlling heart pumping, breathing, etc.

Brain Stem

  • Consists of medulla, pons, and midbrain

Brain Regions

Brain Regions

  • Medulla and Pons
    • Breathing, muscle tone, blood pressure, sleep, arousal
  • Tectum (midbrain)
    • Composed of inferior/superior colliculus
    • Eye movement, visual/auditory reflexes
  • Tegmentum (midbrain)
    • Muscle reflexes, pain perception, breathing
  • Cerebellum
    • Coordination of movements

Brain Regions

  • Thalamus
    • Relay station that conveys info from sensory organs to neocortex
    • Also has active feedback loops with neocortex
  • Hypothalamus
    • Regulates basic needs: feeding, fighting, fleeing, and mating
  • Basal Ganglia
    • Motor control and action selection
  • Amygdala
    • Regulation of emotion
  • Hippocampus
    • Memory, learning, spatial cognition

Neocortex

  • Convoluted surface residing at top of the brain
  • 1/8 inch thick
  • 30 billion neurons, arranged in 6 layers, each neuron making about 10,000 synapses with other neurons, yielding around 300 trillion total connections
  • Exhibits functional specialization
  • Different areas of the cortex are anatomically similar
    • Cortex must employ a prototypical algorithm for processing information
    • Specialization occurs through different inputs received in different brain regions

Chapter 3

Recording and Stimulating the Brain

Recording Signals

Invasive Techniques

Invasive Techniques: Main Points

  • Recordings from individual neurons are typically invasive
  • Based on electrodes and surgically implanting chips
  • Major advantage: allow recording of action potentials at millisecond timescale
    • Non-invasive- hundreds of milliseconds

Animal Subjects

  • Typically animal subjects in vivo
  • Surgery and recovery causes pain and brings risk of disease
  • If awake, restrained during recording
  • Can be recorded for weeks to months

Human Subject

  • Only in clinical setting, such as brain surgery
  • Also used to monitor (still clinical setting) potential risks such as seizures
  • Can be recorded for minutes to hours

Microelectrodes

  • Fine wire or other conductor used to contact brain tissue
  • Made of tungsten/platinum-iridium alloy
  • Insulated except tip
    • Tip ~1μm diameter

http://www.thomasrecording.com/neuroscience-products/chronic-recording-systems/primate-chronic-recording-system/

Intracellular Recording

  • Measure voltage across membrane
  • Most common method: patch clamp recording
  • Delicate, thus typically in vitro vs in vivo

Patch Clamp Recording

  • Microelectrode is a glass pipette filled with weak electrolyte
    • Electrolyte mimics intracellular fluid
    • Tip ~1μm
  • A silver wire connects the electrolyte to an amplifier
  • Voltage is measured with respect to extracellular fluid (reference electrode)

“Brain Computer Interfacing.” By Rajesh P. N. Rao

Extracellular Recording

  • Especially on intact brains of animals
  • Records a single neuron
  • Microelectrode
    • Still tungsten/platinum-iridium tip
    • <10μm tip diameter
  • ​Done with respect to ground (typically a wire on a skull screw)

Extracellular Recording

  • Bring the microelectrode close to the cell body, it will pick up electrical fluctuations
  • Extracellular fluid composition influenced by intracellular changes, thus potential changes
  • Signal is taken and filtered to isolate spikes

“Brain Computer Interfacing.” By Rajesh P. N. Rao

Tetrodes and Multi-Unit Recording

  • Multiple neurons correspond to 1 electrode
  • 4 wires tied tightly in a bundle
  • Will record action potentials in an area
  • Based on a neuron's distance to each wire, has different "signature"
  • ~20 neurons

Tetrode

Multielectrode Arrays

  • Grid of microelectrodes, m x n array where dimensions are 1-10
  • Potentially 10%-50% less neurons than electrodes due to electrode failure
  • Advantages:
    • Gathers information from a larger number of neurons
    • Increased spatial resolution and record from several dozens of neurons to get complex data
  • Disadvantages
    • ​Formation of glial cells and scar tissue in brain
    • Glial cells impede signal and breaks system

Microwire Array

  • Tungsten/platinum alloy/steel electrodes
  • Similar to tetrodes: bundle to find locaiton

http://glo-bio.com/image/cache/data/Array%20and%20Array%20Insertion%20tool/MWA_header-06-15-3_web-500x500.jpg

Michigan Array

  • Has multiple probes along electrode for better readings

Utah Array

  • Only has probe at tip
  • 3D grid

Silicon-based Array

  • Silicon can be shaped more precisely and has higher stiffness
  • Better spatial resolution than microwire

Flexible Array

  • Polyimide, parylene, or benzocylcobutere instead of silicon
  • Material better matches brain tissue to avoid damaging the brain and growing scar tissue

Electrocoticography (ECoG)

  • Placing electrodes on surface of brain
  • Implant grid/strip of array of electrodes
  • Measure tens of thousands of neurons at a time

Advantages and Disadvantages

  • Advantages
    • Safer than implants:
      • No glial tissue
      • No breaking blood-brain boundary
  • Disadvantages
    • Only works in clinical setting
    • Only surgically relevant parts of the head can be recorded
    • Drug and/or patient initial condition.

MicroECoG

  • ECoG but smaller
  • Fraction of mm tip
  • 2-3 mm apart in the grid

Optical Recording: Voltage-Sensitive Dyes

  • Electric activity changes dye's absorption/fluorescence
  • Photodiode arrays are affected simultaneously by many neurons
  • Useful for macroscopic observations, but as so many neurons have a role in the electrode's output, no classification can be done

Optical Recording: 2-Photon Calcium Imaging

  • Calcium corresponds to activity in neuron
  • Steps:
    • 1. Pressure eject load neurons with calcium indicator dyes
    • 2. Monitoring changes in flourescence via 2-photon microscopy (infrared laser beam through objective lens)

Recording Signals

Non-Invasive Techniques

Electroencephalography (EEG):

  • electrodes placed at scalp to record signals
  • reflection of sum of postsynaptic potentials
  • cannot record signals from tangential to scalp/deep inside the brain
  • poor spatial resolution (cm), great temporal resolution (ms)

Conditions

  • weak signals, need amplification/processing and avoid movements
  • cap with recording electrode along with position references: mastoids (behind ear), nasion (on top of nose), inion (base of skull) -- to measure skull circumference​

The 10-20 System

A standard method used to identify locations of the various lobes of the brain with respect to each other

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10-20_system_(EEG)#/media/File:21_electrodes_of_International_10-20_system_for_EEG.svg

EEG Methodology

  • Bipolar (measure potential difference of electrodes) or unipolar (compare charge to neutral or average)
  • one plugs in to differential amplifier and the other to reference electrode, amplifying by 1000-100000 times
  • anti-aliasing filter then A/D (analog to digital), then another filter (1-50 Hz)
  • can capture "brain waves" at various frequencies
  • neurons have characteristic frequency ranges
    i.e. alpha waves - 8-13 Hz in occipital region of awake person when relaxed
    beta waves, delta waves, gamma waves

Magnetoencephalography (MEG)

  • measures magnetic field of brain via superconducting quantum interfacing devices (SQUID)
  • only sensitive to the neurons tangential to scalp due to Maxwell's equations
  • also high temporal resolution
  • offers better spatial resolution than EEG
  • independence from head geometry
  • more expensive, non-portable

Maxwell's Equations of the Brain

  • The neural stimuli in a specific direction as a result of an applied magnetic field

https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0e/Magnetoencephalography.png

Functional Magnetic Resonance Imaging (fMRI)

  • detect change in blood flow from activation of neurons during specific tasks
  • deoxygenated hemoglobin is more magnetic
  • blood oxygenation level dependent (BOLD) response.
  • present stimuli to subjects
  • high spatial resolution, low temporal resolution

Functional Near Infrared (fNIR) Imaging

  • detect near-infrared light absorbance of hemoglobin in blood (comparing oxygenated or deoxygenated)
  • can penetrate skull and reflect back to detectors
  • subjects aren't as strict with movements because of optical measurements rather than electrical

Positron Emission Tomography (PET)

  • measures emission of radioactively labeled chemical injected into blood stream transporting to brain (radiotracer)
  • great spatial resolution, low temporal resolution
  • drawback requires injection of radioactive chemicals and also have to account for rapid decay of radioactivity

Stimulating the Brain

Invasive Techniques

Microelectrodes

  • glass microelectrodes can inject current into cell to excite or inhibit action potentials
  • platinum-iridium microelectrodes can also be used
  • can be used for DBS and can relieve tremors and gait problems

Direct Cortical Electrical Simulation (DCES)

  • electrodes used on the surface of the cortex much like ECoG
  • electric current (<15 mA) delivered across bipolar electrodes
  • can generate movements or cause particular sensations, or do the exact opposite.
  • helps locate where each brain function is located

Optical Simulation

  • two photon laser illumination- illuminate the tangent at the membrane of the cell and creates excitation
  • optogenetic stimulation - use genetic manipulation to make only certain neurons responsive to illumination
  • two photons can selectively excite single neurons while optogenetic can selectively excite a class of neurons

Stimulating the Brain

Non-Invasive Techniques

Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS)

  • uses electromagnetic induction and generates a magnetic field to create a current in the brain and excite the neurons
  • penetrates 3-5 cm into the brain- only for superficial layers
  • advantage is non-invasive

https://cdn.psychologytoday.com/sites/default/files/styles/article-inline-half/public/blogs/84335/2014/01/142724-144052.png?itok=ClZ4n5NL

Transcranial Ultrasound

  • low-intensity pulsed ultrasound to influence neural activation without thermal effects or tissue damage
  • worked up to 92% of mice tested

Simultaneous Recording and Stimulation

Multielectrode Array

  • Good for both recording spike activity and delivering currents to excite or inhibit neurons
  • Some can be used specifically for recording, others for stimulation

Neurochip

  • integrated chip to serve multiple purposes
  • records from neurons, performs signal processing and delivers appropriate stimulation to other neurons
  • preprocessor to do spike sorting
  • instruct circuit to deliver electrical pulses
