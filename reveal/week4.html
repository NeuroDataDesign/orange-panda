Local Motor Potential

  • Correlated with position of individual fingers
    • During grasping motion
  • ECoG signals
    • Low-pass filtered
    • 2 sec moving average window
    • Obtain LMP for each electrode
  • Linear filter
    • Predict each PC of hand motion from LMP

Results

  • LMP extracted from ECoG can be used for:
    • Opening / closing hand
    • Individual finger position
  • Filters trained on data
    • Robust performance across multiple sessions and days
    • Invariant to:
      • Wrist angle / Elbow flex / Hand Placement

Long-Term Stability

  • Performance remained robust over 5 day span
  • Indicates:
    • BCI can be operated using fixed set of params.
    • No need for pre-session adaptation of params.

Nerve Based BCIs

Main Concept

  • Severed peripheral motory nerves still send signals
  • Severed sensory nerves still receive
  • Essentially provides a naturally closed-loop feedback

Median-Nerve Based BCIs

  • Warwick, 2003
  • 100 needle electrodes implanted into median nerve fibers
  • 20 active electrodes read potential + stimulated
  • Ran multiple experiments
    • One received touch feedback via sensors and gripped different objects with a prosthetic
    • One allowed wheelchair control via open + closing hand

More Advanced Experiments

  • Dhillon and Horch, 2005
  • Electrodes in 6 humans with upper limb amputations (at or below elbow)
  • Calibration:
    • Short pulses sent to electrodes to find most effective ones for sensory activity
    • Connected the electrodes to a speaker to isolate which electrode got the highest motor output
  • Started with cursor control training, then moved to arm control, which saw users being able to accurately judge position and angle as well as grip-force

Targeted Muscle Reinnervation

  • Use EMG signals to transmit signals to a prosthetic
  • However, when a muscle is severed, there's no EMG signal to listen to
  • This reconnects severed muscle nerves to currently existing ones, and amplify the signal sent to the new location to control a prosthetic
  • Sensory signals can be routed to skin

Example Experiment: Kuiken, 2007

  • Left arm amputated patient
  • Multiple muscle nerve endings in left arm were rerouted to chest

Rerouting of Nerves and Placement of Electrodes

Addition of BCI

  • After 3 months of rerouting nerves, imagining movement of hand and elbows would make chest twitch
  • 6 months- discernable EMG pattern was found via electrodes, routed signals to prosthetic arm
  • Intuitive control

Noninvasive BCI

Electroencephalographic (EEG) BCIs

  • Records voltage fluctuations from a population of neurons
  • Stronger temporally than spatially, but tech has improved
  • Asynchronous- voluntary signal sent via patient
  • Synchronous- monitoring for a generic stereotype signal for a specific situation

Oscillatory Potentials + ERD

  • Imagery-based BCI: control specific brain rhythms as oscillatory EEG potentials at specific frequencies
  • Performing/imagining movement cause decrease in power of low frequency bands
    • Mu (8-12 Hz)
    • Beta (13-30 Hz)
    • Called Event Related Desynchronization
  • By initially relating BCI control to imagery/movement, eventually train brain

Wadsworth BCI

  • Trained 4 subjects to use mu band to move a cursor up or down
  • Find bipolar spatial filtering based on 2 electrodes
  • Amplitude of mu rhythm :
    • Calculated square root of power at 9 Hz band
    • Compared amplitude to 5 amplitude ranges to decide movement
  • Initially subjects trained only moving up for better control
  • Initially had to rely on imagery for movement, but eventually lost need
  • Accuracy from 80% - 95%

Experiment Results

Wadsworth- 2 Dimensions

  • Same approach but 2 channels of bipolar EEG
    • Left and right hemisphere
    • Sum of left and right generate vertical movement amplitude
    • Difference generate horizontal
  • Equation adjusted to optimize subject experience
  • Achieved accuracy 2-3 times chance levels

Experiment Setup

Comparing with Invasive BCI

  • To compare with the invasive experiments on primates, varied the 2-dimensional experiment
    • Had to hit 8 targets
    • 64 electrodes (split evenly across the head)
  • Adapted weights in a linear model (different weights for left and right) and combined these with a slope weight and intercepts

Results

  • 10 Seconds to Hit Target:
    • 89%, 70%, 78%, and 92% accuracy
    • 1.9s, 3.9s, 3.3s, 1.9s average hit times
  • Comparison with invasive:
    • Similar movement time and hit rate
    • Target sizes were in middle of other primate target sizes

Graz BCI

  • Lower frequency oscillation ERDs, just like Wadsworth
  • However, focus on feature extraction and classification techniques
  • Early prototypes: Find EEG patterns during willful movements
    • Adapt electrode position, frequency band, etc. for each subject to increase accuracy
  • Showed imagery activates same areas as actual movement

Further Studies

  • Continuous feedback of classification performance
    • Bar moved left and right as subject imagined moving right and left hand
  • 3 signal processing methods used:
    • Band power in pre-defined bands
    • Adaptive Autoregression estimated at each iteration
    • CSP (Common Spatial Patterns)
  • Feature vectors classified via LDA
  • Lowest error via CSP, then AAR, worst with band power

Slow Cortical Potentials

  • Reminder: slow non-movement related changes in EEG amplitude
  • SCPs can voluntarily be regulated based on feedback, thus can be used as a "Thought Translation Device" (TTD)
  • Taught 13 healthy and 3 totally motor paralyzed patients to control cursors via SCP
  • Results (of healthy):
    • 4 were able to induce positive responses
    • 3 could induce negative
    • 3 could induce both
    • (Positive and negatives were combined to move left/right and up/down)

Movement-Related Potential

  • MRPs are small and slow potential drift before a voluntary movement
  • the potentials are different between left and right arm, so it's easy to distinguish left vs right movement intention.
  • harder detection than alpha and beta rhythms
  • however, can be more specific to supplementary and primary motor cortical areas

Experimentation

  • Hiraiwa (1990)- backpropagation neural network to test voluntary utterance of vowels (a, e, i, o, u) and moving the joystick in 4 directions
  • 53% patterns correctly classified for vowels, 96% patterns recognized by directions
  • vowels require a higher level of cognitive activity, hence a harder pattern recognition
  • asyncrhonous switch- BCI can detect from a user is idle to active control state
  • low-frequency AS- record 1-4 Hz range

More Experimentation!

  • Shenoy and Rao (2005) - dynamic Bayesian network
  • probability distributions over brain and body-states during planning + execution
  • continuous tracking, allows generation of control signals
  • MRPs can estimate movement

Stimulus-Evoked Potentials

  • stereotypical EEG responses but by specific stimuli
  • rare stimulus triggers a P300 response (300 ms)
  • The P300 response stimulus itself is unpredictable, but is always 300 ms after the stimulus.
  • Generally in the parietal lobe, but sometimes certain components in the temporal and frontal lobes as well.
  • oddball paradigm- spelling a word using a matrix of letters and are flashed in random orders.

Every time a letter flashed, the brain will generate a P300 reponse.

Steady State Visually Evoked Potential

  • detecting SSVEP caused by a continuously fluctuating stimuli (hence the name steady state)
  • Ex: decoding one of two possible choices, these two choices can be represented by a light at different frequency. Then we can see the difference in stimuli.
  • Each button was lit up at a different frequency ranging from 6-14 Hz. Therefore, decoding SSVEP is possible.
  • 8 out of 13 subjects used SSVEP subject to type desire phone number. Avg: 27.15 bpm for ITR.

Auditory Evoked Potentials

  • Donchin and colleagues- used P300 in spoken commands (yes, no, pass, and end), achieved accuracy in 63%-80% in 3 ALS pateitns
  • Hill and colleagues (2005)- ICA with support vector machines. 50 ms square-wave beeps of different frequencies either on the left or right ear. Subjects count the amount of beep on the left or right side.

More on Auditory Evoked Potentials

  • Furdea and colleagues: letters in matrix coded with acoustically presented numbers
  • Less accurate than a visual BCI- Halder (2010) found that ITR has an avg of 2.46 bpm and accuracy around 78.5%.
  • Only reliable to patients who have lost all motor function and have short attention spans.

BCIs based on Cognitive Tasks

  • requires higher levels of thinking, but each cognitive task can be mapped to a control signal
  • can discriminate between activity patterns of different tasks
  • Anderson (1996)- (1) relax, (2) letter composition (3) math, (4) visual counting, (5) geometric figure
  • EEG recorded from 10-20 system. Autoregressive model with two/three-layer backpropagation neural networks

BCIs based on Cognitive Tasks

  • average accuracy ranged from 71% to 38%
  • Galan (2008) - 3 mental tasks to operate simulated wheelchair along specified path. (1) mentally search for words, (2) relaxing while fixating on the center of screen, (3) motor imagery of left hand
  • used LDA classifier. 100% and 80% (different subjects) of the final goals.

Error Potentials in BCIs

  • error detection is very important
  • detection is done by recognizing the brain's reaction to error: hence the error potential
  • Buttfield (2006) - deliberately make 20% errors to record ErrPs from frontocentral region.
  • with errors - accuracy 79.9%, without errors- accuracy 82.4%

Coadaptive BCI

  • BCI can adapt to a user's brain signals continuously
  • non-stationary learning task - continuously mapping brain signals to control signals
  • 3-6 minutes of adaptation go a long way
  • Buttfield (2006) - continuously adapting mean and covariance to calculate the learning rates and online adaptation is better than static (for up to 20.3%)
  • DiGiovanna (2009) tried operant condition with BCI

Hierarchy BCI

  • Continuous non-invasive BCI might be cognitively taxing
  • hierarchical BCI - user teaches BCI system new skills, then invoked directly as high level command
  • developed to control a humanoid robot
  • hierarchical control is faster and more accurate

Other Non-Invasive BCIs

fMRI

  • Can a subject learn to control their blood oxygen level dependent (BOLD) response?
  • Weiskopf (2003) - feedback paradigm- local BOLD signals constantly provided with a delay < 2 sec
  • subjects can increase or decrease BOLD responses

Magnetoencephalography (MEG)

  • higher spatiotemporal resolution than EEG
  • Mellinger (2007) - investigated a MEG on voluntary amplitude modulation of sensorimotor mu and beta rhythm
    • spatial filtering method based on geometric properties of signal propagation
    • successfully communicate limb movements

fNIR

  • captures hemodynamic response
  • Coyle (2004) - fNIR detects characteristic repsonses when subjects engage in motor imagery and utilize this response to control an application
  • easy for drawing and cursor control
  • however, average completion time actually decreased with practice
